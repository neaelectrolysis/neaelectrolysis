Electrologist Survey (United States) 2025 Results

Updated April 2025

The survey is still open. Click here if you're an electrologist and wish to participate. It takes about 10-15 minutes & submissions are anonymous unless you choose to share your email at the end.

Start Up Business Expenses & More

Participants were asked to share of what their business start up expenses were. School tuition was excluded.

18.2% reported $5,000 to $10,000
36.4% reported $10,000 to $15,000
27.3% reported $15,000 to $20,000
18.2% reported $20,000 or more

Next, participants were asked to share their thoughts on how difficult it was to reach their clientele/income goal, based on a scale as shown below.

45% reported Very Easy
91% reported Easy
36.4% reported Neutral
9.1% reported Difficult
0% reported Very Difficult

Participants were also asked to share how long it took for them to reach their clientele/income goal.

36.4% reported 3-6 months
27.3% reported 6-12 months
18.2% reported over 1 year
18.2% reported over 2 years


Business Income & Expenses

Participants were asked to share their estimated NET annual income.

Of the 11 participants, 6 reported a net annual income of $70,000-100,000. One reported $18,000, one reported $95,000, one reported $55,000, one reported $114,000, one stated they had opened their business this year so did not have an annual income to report.

Next, participants were asked to share an estimate of their ongoing business expenses. This includes expenses such as office rent, supplies, etc. Excluding loans. It's unclear if taxes were excluded.

18.2% reported $1,000/month
36.4% reported $1,500/month
27.3% reported $2,000/month
18.2% reported $3,000/month





Participant Demographics

As of April 2025, the survey has only received 11 submissions. There is no publicly available data on how many electrologists are located in the United States.

Two participants stated they are located in Maryland, two reported they are located in California, the remaining participants reported as (one) located in Illinois, Colorado, Washington, Oregon, Ohio.

One participant reported as being located in "Ncc". It's unclear what Ncc stands for, so this participant was excluded from the map.

Of the 11 participants, 10 reported as employed and 1 reported as employed under employer/supervisor.


Reported Work Hours

Participants were asked to report on the hours they work in various contexts.

First participants were asked to share an estimated average of how many hours they've worked (as in performing electrolysis- hours spent on other job tasks excluded) per week in the past 2 months.

27.3% reported 10-20 hrs/week
36.4% reported 20-30 hrs/week
36.4% reported 30+ hrs/week


Next, clients were asked to share an estimate of how many hours they've spent per week on other job tasks, such as marketing and cleaning equipment.

54.6% reported 5 hrs/week or less
27.3% reported 10 hrs/week or less
9.1% reported 10 to 15 hrs/week

Next, participants were asked to share an estimate of how many individual clients they serve, on average, per month.

9.1% reported 10 clients
45.5% reported 30-45 clients
36.4% reported 100-150 clients



































































































Comments by Electrologists

Participants were asked to share their thoughts on the job growth & demand for electrologists in the US, based on their subjective experience & observations. All 11 of the participants responded, and all responses regarded the job growth and/or demand as being positive.

"Very high demand, many of us have been desperate for help for 5+ years now."

"Severe demand, most of us are booked and have waiting lists."

"I currently have a waitlist of about 20 people for starting electrolysis."

"I am the only electrologist in my area. Bigger cities have them but I’m in a college town. I have so many clients I recently put a hold on accepting new clients. I would say this is a huge growing career and many people are seeking it!"

"Wild growth"

"We are all stressed…. Shortage of electrologists in all of the big cities."

"Severe demand, most of us are booked and have waiting lists."

Participants were also asked for their input on job satisfaction, and if they would encourage others to seek electrology as a career investment.

"Yes, I love it!"

"Heck yah!!"

"Yes, if you are skilled at it."

"Yes. It has been a rewarding experience helping someone eliminate a source of anxiety, pain, embarrassment, and anguish."

"Yes. More need to join the field. Growing demand between PCOS and trans women."

"The demand is very high and only increasing."

"I have struggled with unwanted hair growth for the past many years. This has helped boost my confidence as well as all of my clients. It’s very satisfying to see the results and help people become who they truly are and want to be!"

"Absolutely. I love being self-employed, my job is rewarding and not stressful, I feel like I thrive every day at work. Sometimes it’s difficult to deal with all the different personalities, especially if you aren’t great at setting boundaries, but most people are wonderful."

"One has to be passionate about doing electrolysis and they need a mentor after they get their certificate. Many give up because they don't have a mentor. Technically, electrolysis is a harder skill to perfect well."

"I think it’s a great career, very satisfying to help people feel better about themselves. I think you have to remember when you own your own business, you’re making money minus the benefits. If you can find a good job as a w2 employee with good benefits, which is rare in Portland but common in Southern California, you’d be great. Also remember how important it is to take care of your body in this career. Your eyes, your back, your hands. You’re sitting all day every day. Also remember that you can do this for 10 or 15 years and pivot. It’s okay to change careers at different times in your life. But Electrolysis is a great career and I say go for it!"
